FIGURE 06

Is ESG performance a firm-level choice — or is it structurally determined by sector?

Every company plotted as an individual dot, revealing two distinct performance clusters with a near-empty middle ground.

With only 20 companies, a histogram obscures more than it reveals. A dot strip plot shows the full picture: there are effectively two ESG universes — a high-scoring cluster (Technology, Healthcare, Industrials scoring 65–85) and a low-scoring cluster (Energy, Materials scoring 25–36) — with very few companies in the 37–62 range. This gap maps almost perfectly onto sector boundaries, suggesting that sector affiliation is a stronger predictor of ESG performance than any individual management decision.

FIGURE 07 · MACHINE LEARNING

What do unsupervised ML models reveal that manual scoring misses?

PCA-derived pillar weights vs assumed weights · K-Means archetype clustering (k=2, silhouette=0.593)

Two ML techniques reframe the analysis. First, Principal Component Analysis on all 15 raw metrics derives pillar weights statistically — PC1 (53.7% variance) loads heaviest on Environmental metrics, PC2 (22.7%) on Governance, PC3 (6.6%) on Social. This shifts the weighting to E:64.7% G:27.3% S:8.0%, significantly upweighting Environmental and revealing that Social metrics contribute far less independent signal than assumed. Second, K-Means clustering (optimal k=2, silhouette score 0.593) discovers two natural archetypes in the data: Balanced Leaders (12 companies, avg ESG 79.6) and Structural Laggards (8 companies, avg ESG 35.3). The clean separation confirms the polarisation finding from Figure 06 is statistically robust, not a visual artefact.

Left →PCA weights vs assumed weights — bar comparison | Right →K-Means clusters plotted on E vs G axes — bubble size = Social score | Silhouette →0.593 (strong separation)
PCA finding
Environmental metrics explain 53.7% of variance in PC1 — nearly double the assumed 40% weight. Social metrics contribute only 6.6% independent signal (PC3).
Clustering finding
K-Means identifies two statistically distinct archetypes with a 44-point gap in average ESG score — confirming the sector-structural polarisation is not a scoring artefact.
Score impact
PCA reweighting moves scores by up to ±7.4 points. Energy companies score lower under PCA; high-Environmental tech companies score higher — the gap widens.
